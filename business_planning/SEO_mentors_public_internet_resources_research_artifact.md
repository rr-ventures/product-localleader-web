## PHASE 1: AUDIT (Always Start Here)

### Off-Site Audit — Backlinks First
Always audit the backlink profile BEFORE touching on-site content. Backlinks determine whether your technical work will ever matter.

**The 6 Backlink Audit Checks:**

1. **Quality Check** — Are existing links from real, indexed, topically-relevant websites? Red flags: web 2.0 networks (Blogger, WordPress.com subdomains), PBNs, mass dofollow blog comments, sitewide footer links, links from sites with no organic traffic
2. **Relevancy Pyramid Check** — Use the Relevancy Pyramid: top tier = topically AND geographically relevant (e.g., local business association in your niche). Middle tier = topically relevant. Bottom tier = general authority domains (Forbes, Wikipedia). Prioritise top-tier first, always work downward
3. **Right Pages Check** — Are links pointing to the right pages? Most links should naturally hit INFORMATIONAL content, not transactional pages. Transactional pages ("HVAC repair Denver") are difficult to earn links to naturally. Informational assets ("Complete Guide to HVAC Maintenance") earn links and then pass equity to transactional pages via internal links
4. **Velocity Check** — Is there ongoing link acquisition? Stagnant or shrinking link profiles are a trust decay signal to Google. Consistent velocity, even just a few quality links per month, matters more than a burst followed by nothing
5. **Anchor Text Check** — Is anchor text over-optimised? A profile of mostly exact-match keyword anchors ("Denver HVAC repair") is a manual penalty risk. A natural distribution = branded (50%+), URL, generic (click here, learn more), partial match, exact match (small %). Diversify anchors by default
6. **Dilution Check** — Do low-quality links dominate? Do NOT mass-disavow or bulk-remove spammy links — removing links reduces total link equity and often drops rankings. Fix by acquiring new high-quality links to dilute the percentage of bad ones

### On-Site Audit — 4 Priority Areas

1. **Keyword Targeting** — Are you targeting the right keywords? Use Ahrefs or SEMrush to qualify keywords before committing resources. Check: monthly search volume, keyword difficulty, top-ranking page word count, whether ranking pages have backlinks (if yes = you need backlinks to compete)
2. **Site Architecture** — Pages should be within 3–4 clicks of the homepage. Deeper than 4 clicks = significantly reduced crawl value and PageRank flow. Internal links are votes — every internal link passes equity. Deliberately route equity to your most important pages
3. **Content Issues** — Thin content (low word count OR regurgitation of what already exists without unique insight), duplicate content (even minor variations), keyword cannibalization (two or more pages targeting the same primary keyword = they compete against each other and both lose), over-optimization (keyword density that reads as stuffing)
4. **Technical Priorities in Order** — Crawlability and indexation first (use Google Search Console > Coverage), mobile-friendliness, page speed (target under 3 seconds), 302 redirects (always convert to 301), redirect chains (A→B→C should be A→C), broken internal links, missing or duplicate title tags and meta descriptions

## PHASE 2: ON-SITE OPTIMIZATION (4 Core Techniques)

### The Phantom Technique
Add "best" or the current year to title tags across your site. This single change captures high-intent modifier-based searches that most sites completely miss. A page titled "HVAC Services in Denver" becomes "Best HVAC Services in Denver 2026." Apply universally to every important page. Run this audit quarterly and update the year.

### The Tunnel Vision Technique
Target ONE keyword per campaign at a time. Allocate ALL link-building, content, and optimization resources to dominating that single phrase. Once it hits a top 5 position and holds, move to the next keyword. Spreading resources across 10 keywords simultaneously produces mediocre results everywhere and excellence nowhere. Patience and focus beat scattered effort every time.

### The Spider Food Technique
Google crawls top-level pages most frequently. Pages buried 4+ clicks deep receive minimal crawl attention. Fix this by pinning your most important pages higher in site architecture, creating hub pages (pillar pages) that link out to 8–12 related pages, and ensuring every important page is accessible within 2 clicks. A well-structured hub page distributes crawl equity and PageRank to all pages it links to.

### The Authority Transfer Technique (Execute This on Every New Page You Publish)
1. Go to Ahrefs → your domain → "Best by Links" report
2. Identify pages that already have backlinks pointing to them (your link equity holders)
3. Add internal links FROM those already-linked pages TO your target new page
4. Use exact-match anchor text for internal links — this is safe to do unlike external anchors
5. The link equity flows from established pages to new pages, accelerating their ability to rank
6. Do this every single time you publish a new SEO page. It takes 5 minutes and compounds over time

## PHASE 3: CONTENT CREATION

### The Topical Authority Model
Google rewards sites that demonstrate comprehensive expertise on a topic. Random blog posts do not build authority. Clustered topical coverage does.

**Content Architecture:**
- **Pillar pages** — broad, comprehensive coverage of a main topic (2,000–5,000+ words). These are your authority hubs
- **Cluster pages** — specific, narrower subtopics that link back to the pillar. These capture long-tail queries and reinforce the pillar's topical authority
- **Transactional pages** — money pages targeting commercial intent ("HVAC repair Denver"). Linked to from pillar pages, not orphaned
- Cover a topic so completely that a user never needs to leave your site to get the full picture. That is topical authority

### Linkable Assets (The Reverse Silo Strategy)
Create content designed specifically to attract backlinks from external sites. This is the most sustainable link acquisition strategy because the links come to you.

**What makes an asset linkable:**
- Original research, surveys, or data studies (other sites cite your data)
- Definitive guides on topics relevant to your industry
- Visual assets (infographics, calculators, tools)
- Industry statistics roundups (link magnets forever)

**How to verify a topic is linkable BEFORE creating it:**
- In Ahrefs Keyword Explorer, check if the pages currently ranking for your target keyword have external backlinks. If yes → the topic is linkable. If competing pages have zero links → the topic likely won't attract links either

**The Flow:** Linkable informational asset earns backlinks → you add internal links from that asset to your transactional pages → equity flows from the content people link to, to the pages that make you money

### Transactional Page Requirements (Every Money Page Needs All of These)
- **Video** — increases trust, dwell time, and conversion rate. Even a simple explainer video matters
- **Testimonials and micro case studies** — 3–5 customer stories with specific results. Not generic "great service" quotes
- **Educational section** — what should a customer know before hiring you? Answering this positions you as an expert and reduces objections
- **FAQ section** — adds unique content, targets question-based queries, and provides structured content that AI engines cite directly
- **Clear, specific call-to-action** — SEO without conversion infrastructure is wasted traffic

### Multi-Channel Content Strategy (2025–2026 Mandatory Approach)
Build in this exact order to maximise SERP real estate AND LLM retrieval surface area:

1. **Website** — publish and fill topical clusters. This is the foundation
2. **YouTube** — attack the same topics with video. YouTube transcripts are crawled by Google and indexed. Perplexity, ChatGPT, and other LLMs retrieve heavily from YouTube content. A YouTube video on the same topic doubles your retrieval surface
3. **LinkedIn** — posts and LinkedIn Pulse articles. LinkedIn content ranks in Google for professional queries and is indexed by LLMs
4. **Podcast appearances** — interview circuit builds E-E-A-T signals, earns backlinks, and feeds brand entity associations

These three platforms (website + YouTube + LinkedIn) dominate most niches when executed consistently. Most competitors only do one of them.

## PHASE 4: LINK BUILDING (Promotion)

### The Switch-Up Technique (Relationship Starter)
1. Find relevant external links in your content (Wikipedia entries, CNN articles, generic sources)
2. Replace them with links to smaller, relevant blogs and resource pages in your niche
3. Email the blogger to let them know: "Hi [NAME], I was reading about [TOPIC] and linked to your guide on [SUBJECT] from my article at [URL]. Really enjoyed it, thanks for putting it together."
4. This is non-threatening and opens a relationship before you ask for anything. Many will reciprocate naturally. Never ask for a link in the first email.

### The Oprah Technique
Search: "[your niche] + interview" or "interview with [competitor]". Pitch yourself for podcast interviews, expert roundups, and media features. Each interview typically earns at least one editorial backlink from the host site, often more as they promote the episode.

### The Relationship Accelerator Technique
- Host expert roundups on your site (invite 10+ experts to answer one question)
- Interview industry leaders
- Invite guest contributions from known names in your space
- The key is YOU link to THEM first. When you feature others, they naturally promote the content to their audiences, which earns links organically without you ever needing to ask

### The Reverse Guest Post Technique
Invite industry experts to contribute articles TO your site rather than you writing for theirs. Benefits: they bring their audience and promote the content (earning you links), it adds genuine expertise and unique content you didn't have to write, and it avoids keyword cannibalization if you assign topics you haven't covered yet

### The Relevancy Pyramid (Link Sourcing Priority)
When actively pursuing backlinks, prioritise sources in this exact order:
1. **Highest priority** — Topically AND geographically relevant (local business association, regional industry directory, local news coverage of your business)
2. **High priority** — Topically relevant, any geography (industry publications, niche blogs, relevant resource pages)
3. **Medium priority** — General authority domains (business press, news sites)
4. **Low priority** — Generic directories, unrelated sites with high DA
Never chase Domain Authority alone. A link from a regional plumbing association is worth 10x a link from a generic high-DA site if you are a plumbing business

## THE 3 LOCAL RANKING FACTORS (In Order)

### 1. Proximity — Fixed, Cannot Be Changed
How close is the business's verified address to the searcher? A business located in the city centre naturally ranks across a wider radius than one on the outskirts. You cannot change this. Do not waste energy trying to fake proximity with virtual offices unless you have a real, verified physical presence. For most businesses, the goal is to extend the RADIUS of your top-3 coverage outward from your address — not to fake a different address.

### 2. Relevance — Your #1 Lever
Does Google understand exactly what this business does, who it serves, and where it serves them? Relevance is built through:
- GBP categories (what Google officially classifies your business as)
- GBP services (the complete list of things you do)
- Website structure (pages that match and support the GBP's categories and services)
- Content (locally-specific text that proves you operate in specific areas)
- Schema markup (structured data telling Google exactly what your business, services, and locations are)

**Most businesses have critically underbuilt relevance.** A GBP with 1 category and 5 services listed is invisible to Google compared to a GBP with 3 categories and 28 services. The website having no pages matching those categories means the GBP is unsupported and untrusted.

### 3. Authority / Prominence — External Validation
Does Google trust this business based on what the rest of the internet says about it? Built through:
- Citation consistency (NAP: Name, Address, Phone number identical everywhere)
- Quality local backlinks
- Review presence (quantity, rating, recency, owner responses)
- GBP completeness score
- Age and verification history of the GBP

## CRITICAL RANKING RULES (What Ulku Repeats Constantly)

**Rule 1: Position 4 is invisible. Only the top 3 matters.**
The map pack shows 3 results. Position 4 exists below a "More places" link that almost nobody clicks. Your entire strategy should be oriented around achieving and maintaining top-3 positions. A business in position 4 across an entire city is failing regardless of how good their SEO looks on paper.

A plumber with 12 reviews CAN and DOES outrank competitors with 2,800 reviews — this is documented case study evidence from Ulku's agency. **Rule 3: The 4.5–4.8 star sweet spot**
Actively pursue reviews to stay in this range. Below 4.5 hurts conversions and trust. Obsessively chasing a perfect 5.0 is a vanity metric and often looks suspicious to users. A business at 4.7 with 85 reviews converts better than one at 5.0 with 12 reviews.

**Rule 4: Blogs do not rank local businesses on Maps.**
If a local HVAC company publishes 30 blog posts about "how to change your air filter," those articles are not going to move the Maps ranking. Informational blog content targets national/informational queries, not local service queries. The time spent writing blogs should go to building Core 30 pages. **Rule 5: Your website exists to SUPPORT your GBP.**

## GOOGLE BUSINESS PROFILE (GBP) COMPLETE OPTIMIZATION

### Categories (Most Under-Optimised Signal)
- **1 category = critically underbuilt.** Google uses your primary category as the main entity association for your business
- **Target: 2–4 categories** — primary category is the most important service (your main revenue driver), secondary categories add relevance for additional service lines
- Choose secondary categories that your website actually has pages for. If you have no page for "Drain Cleaning" but it's a GBP category, Google cannot verify the claim
- Check competitors in Google Maps using GMB Everywhere (Chrome extension) to see exactly which categories top-ranking businesses use. Copy what works

### Services List (Most Commonly Neglected)
- Most businesses list 5–6 services. This is a critical relevance gap
- **Target: 20–30 services listed** — Google uses the services list to understand the full scope of what you do
- Every service in your GBP must have a corresponding page on your website. The GBP claim → website validation → Google trust
- Services must be exact match with the corresponding website page. If GBP says "Drain Cleaning," the page must say "Drain Cleaning in [City]" — not "Drain Repair" or "Clog Removal" as a title tag substitute

### GBP Description
- Fill it completely. 750 character limit — use all of it
- Include your primary category, city, 2–3 secondary services, a brief authority statement, and a local qualifier (years serving the area, local certifications, neighborhoods served)
- Do NOT stuff keywords. Write it for a human, but include the natural language Google needs to categorise you

### GBP Posts (Activity Signal)
- Post at minimum once per week. Every post signals to Google that the business is active and engaged
- Post types: service spotlights, before/after photos, seasonal offers, community involvement, response to local events
- Posts alone will not rank you, but consistent posting is part of the overall trust signal picture

### GBP Photos (Geotagging + Volume)
- Add photos consistently (weekly ideally). Businesses with regularly updated photos outperform static GBPs
- Geotag your photos before uploading: embed GPS coordinates into the image metadata matching your service area. Free tools (GeoImgr) do this. It sends geographic relevance signals
- Include: team photos, job site photos (with permission), before/after, equipment, and exterior shots
- Photo descriptions should include your primary category and city where natural

### GBP Q&A Section
- Google allows anyone to ask questions in the Q&A section — including you. Seed your own Q&A with the questions customers actually ask
- Pre-populate 5–10 Q&As covering: service areas, pricing, response time, certifications, process, guarantees
- These Q&As are crawled by Google and can appear in AI Overviews for local queries

### GBP Embed on Homepage
- Embed your Google Maps/GBP directly on your homepage using the embed code from Google Maps
- This sends an entity association signal confirming the website and GBP are connected
- Place it visibly on the homepage, not buried in the footer

## WEBSITE STRUCTURE: THE CORE 30 FRAMEWORK (Ulku's Signature System)

This is the foundational content architecture for any local business website. It produces topical AND geographic relevance simultaneously — the two signals Google needs to rank a GBP higher and across a wider area.

### The Exact-Match Rule (Most Important Rule in Local SEO)
The language on your website must **exactly match** the language in your GBP. Not synonyms. Not variations. Exact match.

- If GBP primary category = "Air Conditioning Repair" → Homepage title tag = "Air Conditioning Repair in [City]"
- If GBP service = "Drain Cleaning" → Service page title tag = "Drain Cleaning in [City]"
- If GBP secondary category = "Heating Contractor" → Category page title tag = "Heating Contractor in [City]"

Why: Google validates GBP claims against website signals. If the language doesn't match, the validation fails, and the relevance signal is weakened. This is why businesses rank for one thing but not others — they have partial matches.

### Homepage Structure (Ulku's Formula)

**Title Tag:** `[Primary Category] [City Name]`
Example: "Plumber Houston" or "HVAC Repair Denver Colorado"

**H1 Tag:** Same formula as title tag or close variation
Note: The H1 that matters is the one Google and users actually SEE as the most visually prominent heading. Structure the visual hierarchy so the most important heading is also the H1 in HTML.

**First Paragraph:** 50–100 words including primary category, city, year established or years serving the area, 2–3 service mentions, and a proximity signal (neighborhoods or areas served)

**Homepage Subheadings (H2s for each secondary GBP category):**
List every secondary GBP category as its own H2 section on the homepage. Under each H2, write 50–100 words about that service and link to the dedicated category page.
Example: If your secondary categories are "Drain Cleaning," "Water Heater Installation," and "Sewer Repair" → these are 3 separate H2 sections on the homepage with blurb text and links to their respective category pages.

**This creates the wheel:** Homepage → Category Pages → Service Pages → Back to Homepage via navigation. Internal link structure mirrors GBP structure = perfect entity alignment.

### The Wheel Linking Method
Structure internal linking as a wheel:
- Homepage = hub
- Category pages = spokes off the hub
- Service pages = spokes off each category page
- Every category page links back to homepage
- Every service page links back to its category page and to homepage
- Cross-link related services where contextually natural

This structure ensures every page receives link equity from every other page. Google crawls along these links and builds a clear picture of your business's full service scope.

### Category Pages (One Per GBP Secondary Category)

**Page anatomy:**
- Title tag: `[Secondary Category] [City]`
- H1: Same as title tag
- Opening paragraph: 100–150 words establishing authority in this service category in this city
- H2 subheadings: one per service that falls under this category
- 50–100 words under each H2 linking to the individual service page
- Local FAQ section (5+ questions about this service category)
- Schema: Service schema + LocalBusiness schema
- Minimum 1,000 words total

### Service Pages (One Per GBP Service)

**Page anatomy:**
- Title tag: `[Service Name] in [City]`
- H1: Same as or close variation of title tag
- Content: 800–1,200+ words
- Must include LOCAL flavor (see AI Content section below)
- Process section: what happens when you call / what the service involves
- Benefits section: why hire a professional, risks of DIY
- FAQ section: 5+ questions specific to this service
- Local testimonials section or review embed
- Clear CTA with phone number and contact form
- Schema: Service schema

### Geographic Content (After Core 30 Is Complete)

**Critical sequencing rule (Ulku):**
1. FIRST: Build all Core 30 pages (topical relevance for your primary city)
2. THEN: Achieve 20–40% top-3 coverage across your primary service area (measure this with LeadSnap)
3. ONLY THEN: Expand with geographic content targeting neighborhoods, surrounding cities, and service area pages

**Why this order matters:** Publishing neighborhood content before you rank in your core city is like trying to fill a leaking bucket. The Core 30 is the bucket fix. Geographic expansion is the growth phase.

**Neighbourhood targeting strategy:**
- Use LeadSnap rank tracking to identify grid points where you are ranking at position 4 or 5
- Create geographic content specifically targeting those areas: "[Service] in [Neighbourhood Name]" pages
- These pages pull rankings from position 4–5 into the top 3 by adding geographic specificity
- Reference local landmarks, zip codes, distances from known areas, local events, and community context on each geographic page

**City border ranking walls:**
Rankings drop sharply at city administrative borders regardless of actual distance. To rank in a different city you need: a verified physical address in that city, a verified service area GBP expansion, or a genuine satellite office with local NAP consistency. There is no content workaround for this.

## AI CONTENT: WRITING LOCAL PAGES THAT GOOGLE TRUSTS

This is Ulku's "AI without slop" methodology. AI does 80% of the work, but the remaining 20% is what separates ranking pages from invisible pages.

### The Anti-Slop Requirement
Generic AI content (the kind ChatGPT produces with a basic prompt) reads as interchangeable across any city. Google can detect this through its "helpful content" systems. Pages that could be about "plumbing in Houston" or "plumbing in Chicago" with just the city name swapped are thin and receive little ranking weight.

**What makes content NOT slop:**
- Specific local neighborhood mentions (not just the main city — reference actual neighborhoods, districts, suburbs where your customers live)
- Local weather patterns and how they affect your service (e.g., "Dallas summers push AC systems to their limits — here's what to check before peak season")
- Local building codes and permits relevant to the service
- Local landmarks as proximity references
- Real customer stories from identifiable local areas (anonymised but geographically specific)
- Seasonal relevance to the local climate

### Ulku's AI Prompt Framework for Local Content
Use AI prompts that include the following instructions:
- "Write this for [City], including specific neighborhoods like [list 5-8 local neighborhoods]"
- "Reference [local weather pattern, e.g., 'Houston humidity'] and how it affects [service]"
- "Include a mention of [local landmark or area] as a proximity reference"
- "Add a section about local building codes or permits for [service] in [City]"
- "Write it so someone reading it could only be in [City] — not just a city name swap"

The AI draft then gets reviewed by a local (you, your staff, or a local freelancer) who adds one or two genuine local details that an AI cannot fabricate.

### Super Citations (Ulku's Tier 1 Authority Signals)
Super Citations are listings on high-trust, verified platforms that require actual identity verification to create. These are not mass-directory blasts. They are signals that tell Google, ChatGPT, Apple Maps, Bing, Siri, and Alexa that your business has been validated by trusted external systems.

**Super Citation platforms (target all of these):**
- Bing Places for Business (verified)
- Apple Maps (verified via Apple Business Connect)
- Waze (verified business listing)
- Amazon Alexa / Echo business listings
- Google Maps (verified GBP — your existing one)
- Yelp (verified and claimed)
- Facebook Business Page (claimed and completed)
- BBB (Better Business Bureau — paid but high trust)
- Navigation systems: Here Maps, TomTom, Garmin POI

**Use LeadSnap** to distribute your GBP data to 50+ verified platforms in bulk, then manually verify each one. Do not use cheap citation services — they create inconsistent NAP data, which actively hurts local rankings and confuses AI recommendation systems.

### NAP Consistency — The Foundation of Citation Trust
Name, Address, Phone number must be byte-for-byte identical across every listing, your website, and your GBP. Even minor variations destroy citation trust signals:
- "Suite 4" vs "Ste. 4" — different
- "(555) 123-4567" vs "555-123-4567" — technically different to some parsers
- "LLC" vs "L.L.C" — different
Audit all existing citations with Moz Local or BrightLocal and standardise every instance before building new ones

### Standard Citations (Tier 2)
After super citations are complete, build listings on:
- Industry-specific directories (Houzz for home services, Healthgrades for medical, Avvo for legal, etc.)
- Local business directories (city-specific business associations, local chamber directories)
- Regional news site business listings
These exist to reinforce NAP consistency and add topical + geographic citation signals. Volume matters less than accuracy.

### Old-School Mass Citations — No Longer Recommended (Ulku, 2025)
Mass directory blasts (hundreds of citations across low-quality sites) used to work in 2018–2020. Post-2023 algorithm updates, mass inconsistent citations now actively harm rankings by introducing NAP inconsistency and confusing AI retrieval systems. Do citations only for legitimate, verified, relevant platforms.

## LOCAL LINK BUILDING: THE AUTHORITY LAYER

### Why Local Links Are Different From National Links
A backlink from the local Chamber of Commerce is worth more to a local business's Maps ranking than a link from Forbes. Google's local algorithm weights geographic and topical relevance more heavily than raw domain authority for local pack rankings. A $200 community sponsorship can outperform a $2,500/month SEO retainer in terms of local ranking impact — this is documented in Ulku's DC HVAC case study.

### Anti-Slop Links (Minimum 1 Per Core 30 Page)
Every Core 30 page needs at least one quality local link pointing to it. Without a link, Google has no external validation that the page content is real and trustworthy. Ulku calls this the "anti-slop signal" — it proves to Google that a human or local entity has vouched for the content, distinguishing it from AI-generated filler. This is now mandatory in a post-Helpful Content world.

### The Chamber of Commerce Link (Easiest High-Value Link)
The local Chamber of Commerce membership link is consistently the easiest high-authority local link available:
- Most chambers charge $150–$400/year for membership
- Membership comes with a directory listing on the chamber website
- Chamber websites have high local domain authority and are trusted by Google as genuine local entities
- **Real case study (Ulku):** Client went from position 8 to position 3 overnight after Chamber membership and a community event sponsorship
- This should be the FIRST local link acquisition action for any new client

### Community and Sponsorship Links (High ROI)
- **Youth sports team sponsorships** — Sponsor a local team ($200–$500), get listed on the team or league website. These sites have genuine local authority and natural editorial placement
- **Local charity sponsorships** — Often comes with a press release on a local news site (.localcity.com/news) which is excellent for local authority
- **School events and fundraisers** — .edu-adjacent domain links with genuine community trust
- **TEDx event sponsorships** — $250–$500 entry level. Gets your business on the local TEDx site which has strong domain authority
- **Local event sponsorships** — Farmers markets, festivals, local business association events

### Local Link Audit (Before Building New Links)
- Check which Core 30 pages have zero external links pointing to them (Ahrefs → Best by Links filter)
- Prioritise acquiring links to the service pages most important to your revenue
- One quality local link per page is enough to create the anti-slop validation Google is looking for

## RANK MAPS AND THE TOP-3% METRIC (Ulku's Measurement System)

### LeadSnap Rank Tracking
Do not measure local SEO with average position or keyword ranking reports. Measure with a rank map grid.

LeadSnap (and similar tools like Local Viking, BrightLocal Maps) displays your rankings as a grid of dots across your city. Each dot represents a point in your service area and shows your ranking position at that geographic point:
- **Green dots** = top 3 positions (visible to searchers)
- **Yellow dots** = positions 4–7 (essentially invisible)
- **Red dots** = positions 8+ (completely invisible)

Your goal: turn the entire city grid green.

### The Top-3 Percent Metric
Calculate what percentage of your rank map grid shows top-3 positions. A healthy local SEO campaign should be moving this number consistently upward:
- **0–20% top-3 coverage** = early stage, foundational work not yet complete
- **20–40% top-3 coverage** = solid foundation, time to layer on geographic expansion content
- **40–70% top-3 coverage** = competitive market leader
- **70%+** = dominant player, actively defending territory

### The Expansion Strategy Using Rank Maps
Identify your "border zones" — the grid points where you are at position 4 or 5. These are near-misses. Create specific neighbourhood-targeting pages or geographic service pages for those exact areas. Push rank-4 positions into the top 3 by adding geographic specificity. This is more efficient than trying to improve from position 8.

## SCHEMA MARKUP FOR LOCAL SEO

Think of schema as labelled moving boxes. Without labels, Google has to open every box and guess what's inside. With schema, Google reads the label and immediately understands the content. This speeds up correct categorisation and improves entity understanding.

**Required schema for every local business:**

1. **LocalBusiness schema** — on homepage and every Core 30 page:
   - name, address, telephone, url, geo coordinates, openingHours, priceRange, serviceArea, areaServed
2. **Service schema** — on every service page:
   - name (exact match to GBP service name), provider, areaServed, description
3. **FAQ schema** — on every Core 30 page that has a FAQ section:
   - Enables FAQ rich snippets in Google organic results (takes up more SERP real estate)
   - Also directly feeds Google AI Overviews for question-based queries
4. **Review schema** — aggregate rating markup if you display reviews on-page

Use Google's Rich Results Test after implementing to verify schema is correctly read.

## TECHNICAL FOUNDATIONS FOR LOCAL SEO

### Title Tag Formula (Non-Negotiable)
The most common reason a local business ranks poorly is a bad title tag. Ulku sees this daily:
- "Home | ABC Plumbing" → invisible
- "Welcome to Our Website" → invisible
- "ABC Plumbing Services - Quality You Can Trust" → weak
- **"Plumber in Houston | ABC Plumbing"** → correctly structured

Primary category + city name is the minimum formula. Every important page should follow this.

Real case study: A roofer in Kansas City changed their title tag from "Welcome to Our Roofing Company" to "Roof Replacement Kansas City" and moved from page 3 to page 1 within two weeks. Nothing else changed.

### Page Speed for Local
Local searches are overwhelmingly mobile. A slow mobile page kills conversions and ranking simultaneously. Target:
- Sub-3 second load on mobile (test with Google PageSpeed Insights)
- Core Web Vitals: LCP under 2.5s, CLS under 0.1, FID under 100ms
- Compress all images before uploading (TinyPNG or similar)

### Crawlability
- Submit your sitemap to Google Search Console after publishing Core 30 pages
- Ensure robots.txt is not accidentally blocking important pages
- Use Screaming Frog to audit crawl depth — Core 30 pages should all be within 2–3 clicks of homepage

### Mobile-First Structure
- Google indexes the mobile version of your site first
- All Core 30 pages must be fully readable and navigable on mobile
- Click-to-call phone number must be visible on mobile without scrolling
- Contact form should be above the fold on mobile for service pages

# SECTION E: HOW TO RESPOND TO SEO QUESTIONS

**Step 1: Classify the query**
- Is this LOCAL SEO (Maps pack, local organic) or NATIONAL/INFORMATIONAL SEO (organic rankings, content)?
- What channel is the goal: organic page 1, Maps top 3, AI answer visibility, or all three?

**Step 2: Apply the correct framework**
- Organic page 1 → Gotch 4-phase system (Audit → Optimize → Create → Promote)
- Maps top 3 → Ulku Core 30 system (GBP → Website Structure → Citations → Local Links)
- AI/GEO visibility → Gotch GEO principles (entity associations + structured content + multi-platform)
- Often the answer will use all three systems in combination

**Step 3: Prioritise ruthlessly**
Name the single highest-impact action to take first. What will move the needle most in the shortest time? Be specific: "Change your homepage title tag to [Primary Category] [City] before anything else."

**Step 4: Call out common mistakes**
When the question implies a common misconception, address it directly:
- If someone asks "how do I get more reviews to rank higher" → correct the mistake (reviews ≠ ranking signal)
- If someone asks "should I write blog posts to rank locally" → correct the mistake (blogs ≠ Maps ranking)
- If someone asks "should I add more keywords to my GBP description" → correct (keyword stuffing = weak, structural signals = strong)

**Step 5: Show the math**
When possible, explain the Google scoring logic behind the recommendation. Why does this tactic work according to how Google's algorithm actually processes signals?

**Be opinionated and direct.** These frameworks are battle-tested at 7-figure agency scale across 50+ markets. There is no need to hedge or offer "it depends" when a clear principle applies.
